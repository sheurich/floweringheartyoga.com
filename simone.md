My journey on the path of yoga began unexpectedly in 1971 when a friend's enthusiastic invitation brought me to a local yoga class in Bethlehem Pennsylvania where I lived at the time. Since I don't remember much about the instructor, or about the class itself, I can only imagine how much my awareness and understanding of mySelf has grown throughout this time. I do know I felt some deep connection with yoga and that relationship has continued to grow.

After the classes finished, I continued to practice what I remembered. Within a few years(1972 or 73), I took another series of classes with some of the Swamis from the Kripalu ashram in Sumneytown, Pennsylvania. This would be my first taste of rich yogic philosophy and lifestyle. I was torn between my desire to learn more and my fear of being drawn into something I wasn't ready for. I chose to continue my practice on my own.

In 1975, I moved to Maryland and began searching for yoga classes. I found a wonderful teacher, Swami Lalitananda, who had studied at the Sivananda ashram in the Bahamas. She taught a very gentle form of hatha yoga combined with pranayama, meditation and philosophy. I studied with her for about 8 years. During this time I also attended workshops with visiting teachers including Swami Satchidananda, Swami Vishnu-devananda, and others.

In 1983, I began studying with Kripalu teachers again. I was drawn to their approach which emphasized awareness, acceptance and investigation rather than forcing the body into postures. I attended many workshops and retreats at the Kripalu Center in Lenox, Massachusetts. In 1987, I completed their month-long teacher training program.

Since then, I have continued to study with many wonderful teachers including Judith Hanson Lasater, Patricia Walden, John Friend, Rodney Yee, and others. Each teacher has contributed something valuable to my understanding and practice.

In 1988, I began teaching yoga classes in my home and at various locations in the Hagerstown area. Teaching has been a wonderful way to deepen my own practice and understanding. I am continually amazed by what I learn from my students.

My approach to teaching emphasizes awareness, breath, and honoring the wisdom of the body. I believe that yoga is not about achieving perfect postures, but about developing a loving relationship with ourselves and discovering our own inner wisdom.

I am grateful for this path that has brought so much richness to my life and I feel blessed to be able to share it with others.
